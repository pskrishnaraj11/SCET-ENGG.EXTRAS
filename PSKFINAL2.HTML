WHO IS AN IDEAL ENGINEER?

There are a number of things that are important here: Versatility - Early-stage engineers will often wear a lot of hats. They'll need to do some back-end coding, maybe some front-end coding, some DevOps, etc. They aren't going to have the luxury of having a QA guy to pass things along to, etc. So they need to be able to pick up and run with a lot of stuff. Velocity - They need to be able to move reasonably fast. Speed is of the essence in an early-stage environment. While it's important to also write high-quality code, a "perfectionist" engineer might not always be the greatest fit early. It's often better to get something out the door and in the hands of customers than to make sure that you're building something that can scale to millions of users. Flexibility - Inevitably some of the code you write in an early-stage environment will get thrown away (doing customer development and following Lean Startup principles can help with this). An early-stage engineer must be able to roll with the punches and not get too bent out of shape when the direction of the business changes. Solid communication skills - In larger environments there will be more layers between the business people (who often aren't technical) and the people writing code. In an early-stage environment those layers don't exist. An early-stage engineer who can't communicate well can be problematic. However, the converse is true and the non-engineers in an early-stage environment also need to be able to communicate well with engineers. This may mean getting more technical themselves, picking up a bit of coding, etc. Resilience - Things will go wrong early stage and the world often feels like it is stacked against you. An engineer who bails at the first sign of trouble is not going to do well in an early-stage environment. There needs to a high level of risk tolerance and willingness to fail and keep moving forward.
